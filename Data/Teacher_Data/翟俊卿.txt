在我国，随着博物馆的免费开放，许多家长带着孩子来访参观，学习科学知识，培养科学兴趣。然而，目前我国很少有研究关注以家庭为团体的来访者，尤其是他们在参观过程中如何进行互动沟通，以及这种互动沟通对孩子学习科学的影响。目前为止，我国学者中只有青紫馨和伍新春（2013）对初中生在参观科技馆过程中的对话类型进行了探讨，但是该研究关注的是学生之间的对话而不是学生与家长之间的对话。由于目前我国基础教育尚未将非正式科学教育场馆（如：科技馆、博物馆、植物园、动物园等）作为学校教育的组成部分，而且参观上述场馆的团体以家庭为主，因此本项目以浙江省自然博物馆和浙江科技馆的来访家庭团体（小学生及其家长）为研究对象，通过分析他们在参观过程中的对话，以及参观前后的访谈，探究以下两个问题：（1）孩子与家长之间对话的形式和内容；（2）亲子之间互动沟通对孩子学习科学的影响。本研究的结果将有助于了解非正式科学教育场所中家庭来访团体的学习过程，并为非正式科学教育场馆如何设计展览，增进来访者之间的互动提供参考性建议。 Scientific inquiry and formative assessment in primary science classroom (Aug 2011-Dec 2013)National Institite of Education Nanyang Technological University Singapore(funded by Singapore Millennium Foundation 600000 SGD)    In their highly acclaimed review of 250 studies Black and Wiliam (1998) concluded that formative assessment if appropriate implemented would improve students’ learning. Since then there has been a worldwide emphasis on teachers using assessment to improve students’ learning and achievement (Cooper & Cowie 2010). In science there was also call for changes in science education in the United States (National Science Education Standards NSES 1996). The basis for this is that there should be opportunities for students to develop understanding of not only content knowledge but also skills like problem solving and critical thinking. In science these skills also includes ability to develop hypothesis conduct investigations and draw evidence-based conclusion which OECD defined as the scientific literacy needed to make informed decision about the world around them.To this end NSES identified inquiry as central to science learning. Inquiry-based activities in the classroom allow students to be actively engaged in scientific questions formulate explanations based on evidences and communicate findings. This outlines a change from traditional classroom in which teacher is the authority of knowledge to one in which students collaborate in groups to build new knowledge with teachers as a facilitator. With this shift of authority within the classroom it also means there needs to be a change in how we assess students learning. In fact Harlen (2003) argued that for effective learning science inquiry should be integrated with formative assessment to allow both students and teachers to clarify the status of the students’ learning and also map the route to achieving the learning goals.     The promises that formative assessment holds will not be fulfilled without effective teachers. Studies have shown that teachers’ practices are much influenced by their beliefs (Nespor 1987; McRobbie and Tobin 1995; Tobin and McRobbie 1996; Yerrick Parke et al. 1997; Brown 2004; Lefstein 2008; Mansour 2009; Remesal 2011). Hence in this study the focus is on how primary school teachers make sense of the two new initiatives in the science classrooms by examining teachers’ beliefs and their enact assessment during inquiry-based lessons. Given that a classroom is a social setting which is extremely complex this study is carried out in two phases and adopts a mixed method approach which according to Greene (2007) could gain a better understanding of the multi-faceted character of the phenomena. Pedagogical practices of botanic garden educators (Sept 2007-July 2011)Kings College London UK(Funded by KC Wong Scholarship & Rosalind Driver Studentship 70000 GBP) Although informal outdoor educators play an important role in facilitating pupils cognitive and affective development on school trips the details of their pedagogical practice are not yet clearly understood. This study explores botanic garden educators pedagogical approaches when teaching ecological science to visiting schoolchildren (7-13 years old). More specifically drawing on the sociocultural perspective of learning which highlights the importance of discourse in learning processes it examines the interactions between four botanic garden educators and their visiting schoolchildren focusing on how the pupils contributions to the lesson talk are followed up by the botanic garden educators. Moreover it contains an investigation regarding what kinds of teaching behaviours by botanic garden educators best support pupils learning. The analyses of discourse data collected from eight school visits guided by the botanic garden educators show that the educator-pupil interaction is largely triadic (educator initiation student response and educator feedback) in nature. It also emerged that most of the botanic garden educators questions were closed-ended which fails to foster the childrens productive thinking. Further from the analysis of the botanic garden educators pedagogical behaviours it became apparent that certain approaches such as: "explaining by comparing" "storytelling" and "metaphorical modelling" are effective strategies for communicating ecological science to young pupils. There was much evidence that learning-oriented school visits to botanic gardens could be substantially improved through effective ongoing school-garden collaborations and better education programmes.
